Accounting Rate of Return (ARR)

The Accounting Rate of Return, also known as Return on Investment (ROI), or Return on Capital Employed (ROCE), is the return in profits generated from the employment of capital.

The aim is to determine how much per £ of capital invested was returned in profit. We calculate this with; Avg annual profits / Avg capital invested.

This is where the avg profit is the accounting profit for the life of the project divided by the project length (in years). And where the avg capital invested would be the total amount of assets the company has tied up in the project - this is typically initial investment (or "investment at start") + investments at end divided by 2.

More Information

Initial investment is capital used at the start of the project, like machinery, plant, inventory etc. And investment at end is the accumulation of capital employed throughout the period of the project, like receivables, inventory, cash/equivalents. This is why we add them both at the end of the project, then divide by avg accouting profit to get the ARR.

Financial Statements Correlation

If we consider the income statement and balance sheet, know the link between them both, those being income/expense and assets/liabilities (to keep it simple). Exploring that allows us to expand on what would fall into the accounting profit and investment aspect of ARR calculations.

Income Statement

As we’ve mentioned, we’re using the Accounting Profit when we mention profit in ARR calculations. It would be easy to just say “well yes, it’s in the name” but we can easily uncover why it’s the accounting profit we use.

The accounting profit accounts for the direct income and expenses (cash and non-cash) aspects of the business's activities – which is no different to ARR, except we look at project specifics, not the business as a whole. The accounting profit accounts for the comings and goings financially, but also disregards taxes and interest, while still accounting for the depreciation and amortisation of non-current assets. Therefore, the accounting profit allows us to view everything relevant to the project.

Balance Sheet

Before we proceed, it’s important to note that liabilities are only of consideration when we look at the financing of the project, however, our aim is the return of capital employed. Therefore, as far as the balance sheet goes, we only consider assets – hence the need for including depreciation and amortisation within the profit that we use.

Non-Current Assets

There are a few considerations that need to be made when reviewing the employment of non-current assets. Firstly, we want to determine whether we are employing an asset that we already own, or whether we are purchasing a new one. If we are using one that we already own, the figure added to initial investment is the NBV at the time of the employment. If we are purchasing a new one, it would be the “At Cost”, however, depending on the type of asset, we may also have additional costs, like installing costs, or setup costs.

Secondly, we want to look at leasing options – of which we have Operating Lease (short-term), and Finance Lease (long-term). If we take on an operating lease, the asset legally and economically remains with the lessor, in which case, they account for the depreciation/amortisation, not us. And the lease payments would fall within the accounting profit figure, not capital employed (we don’t own the asset) Alternatively, if we take on a finance lease, the asset economically (not legally) remains with us, in which case – in accordance with IFRS 16 ROU (Right-Of-Use) and lease liability are recognised and we account for depreciation/amortisation, and it is the NBV of the leased asset we add to the initial investment figure upon employment.

It’s worth noting that the reason for the distinction between both lease types is the fact that we economically benefit from the use of the asset, we technically do both, but with finance lease, we use the asset for most of its useful life, then we buy it, be that automatically at the lease end, or through a bargain purchase option.

Current Assets

When we review the use of current assets, some may be part of the initial investment, and other accumulate throughout the project life. For example, inventory and cash/equivalents. When we start a project, we’re likely to start with some net working capital, like cash in inventory and receivables (net of payables). This portion will be within the initial investment figure because it represents capital employed in the project.

As the project proceeds, the level of working capital will vary from increases to decreases. These changes affect the capital employed throughout the project’s life. Therefore, I want us to imagine we are working with working capital like we do inventory – opening and closing balances. The initial investment is the opening, which would include; inventory, receivables, and cash/equivalents. Throughout the year, these vary, and at the end of the project, we have our closing; inventory, receivables and cash/equivalents - we then divide by 2 to get the average investment figure. Note that although non-current assets are not a part of working capital, they too would be in this figure.

Inventory

Before we end there, just a little clarification for inventory. The inventory part of working capital is avg invetntory, which is achieved by added up all the stages of production; raw material, work in progress, and finished goods. Which includes both opening and closing (adding them together), and dividing by 2.

Payback Period

This calculates how long it takes to recover the initial investment cost. Let's give an exercise a go, so that we can illustrate this. Let's Assume we are investing £10 into both Project A, and Project B.

Project A Project B
Cashflow In/-Out Cumulative Cashflow Cashflow In/-Out Cumulative Cashflow
Initial Investment -10 -10 -10 -10
Year 1 2 -8 4.3 -5.7
Year 2 3 -5 5.7 0
Year 3 3 -2 1.7 1.7
Year 4 6 4 1 1
Total Return 14 12.7
Payback 3 Years 4 Months 2 Years

In Project A we receive the following payments; 2 in year 1, 3 in year 2, 3 in year 3, and 6 in year 4. For Project B, it's 4.3 in year 1, 5.7 in year 2, 1.7 in year 3, and 1 in year 4.

Using Project A to explain this simply, we recover 2 of -10 in year 1, leaving -8 to recover. We recover 3 of -8 in year 2, leaving -5. We recover 3 of -5 in year 3, leaving -2. And in year 4 we recover the -2.

Each year, for both 4 year projects, we receive payment for the work we done. Each year, for sake of calculating, we assume payment is made in equal payments. Therefore, to determine the payback period, we want to add up cumulative cashflow to see where we break-even.

For Project A, we can see between years 3 and 4 we make break-even. So we know it’s within the 3 years benchmark, all that’s left is determining the months. We do this with the following calculation (on Excel); = - (2 / 6 ) * 12. This gives us 4. Therefore, in Project A, we recover our initial investment in 3 years and 4 months.

For Project B, we can see clearly that in year 2 we make break-even, however, we can do the same calculation; = - (5.7 / 5.7 ) * 12. This gives is 12. Therefore, in Project B, we recover our initial investment in 2 years.

Additional Information

Drawbacks of Payback

It’s worth noting that we do have some drawbacks of the Payback Period. For example, there is no allowance for the time value of money, i.e., the value of money at the end of the project as it was at the start. Additionally, payback period ignores the cashflow obtained after the break-even point. And all of this comes down to one major drawback – it relies on an arbitrary cut-off meaning that there is no financial principle that we follow, rather it's based on company policy, or management’s directive. For example, they may say not to accept projects with a life cycle of more than 3 years, but why 3, why not 2 and a half year, or 4 years. Altogether, this makes it somewhat difficult to provide evidence to support the use of payback period calculations.

Benefits of Payback

Payback has several benefits, for example, its simplicity. Payback is very easy to implement and understand, making it a good tool to use, in addition to others like ARR. There is also the perceived risk reduction, where we believe that projects that can cover their costs early reduce the firm's exposure. Another would be the focus on liquidity, where if funds are limited, payback highlights projects that return cash sooner, better improving liquidity within the business. Additionally, payback also offers practicality, it can be a great tool for early-stage screening of projects, and act as a supplement to other methods, like ARR (as mentioned). And lastly, there is the indicative value it provides – in theory, because we believe that early return on invesments can give indications to future performance on projects.
