Introduction

Capital investment refers to spending long-term assets that will benefit the business for more than one year. These assets either: generate revenue (e.g., launching a new product line), or reduce cost (e.g., new machinery that cuts labour). The key point is the capital investment requires spending money now and receiving benefits later. This time gap is what makes appraisal techniques essential.

Capital investment is critial because:

  • large Capital Outlay - projects often require substantial upfront spending.
  • No Immediate Gain - cash only goes out in Year 0; benefits arrive years later.
  • Funding Limits - companies always have more potential projects than available money, which leads to capital rationing.
  • Irreversibility - once a factory or machine is purchased, reversing the decision is extremely costly.

The investment process - which is before the appraisal happens, follows a structured organisational process:

  • Search for Opportunities - spotting efficiency improvements, new markets, product innovations.
  • Generation of Ideas - brainstorming alternatives.
  • Development of Proposals - collecting cash flow estimates and technical details.
  • Screening - removing projects misaligned with strategy.
  • Appraisal - using tools like NPV/IRR to financially evaluate.
  • Agreement of Capital Budget - enior management allocates funds.
  • Strategy Alignment - ensuring chosen projects support long-term objectives.

Remember – the underlaying objective is shareholder wealth maximisation – investment appraisal exists to identify projects that increase shareholder value.

Investment Appraisal Methods

Before diving into each method, understand that appraisal techniques are tools to determine whether a project will increase value.

The four key methods are:

  • Accounting Rate of Return (ARR).
  • Payback Period.
  • Net Present Value (NPV).
  • Internal Rate of Return (IRR).

The first two are simpler and do not discount cash flows, but NPV and IRR are more advanced and account for the time value of money.
