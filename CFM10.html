Introduction

A dividend policy is the decision a firm makes about how much of its profits it pays out to shareholders, and how much it retains for reinvestment. The policy concerns the pattern of dividends, not the total amount of shareholders return over time. The key question to be asked is: “can shareholder wealth be increased by altering when dividends are paid (e.g., more not, less later)?”.

Why Do Dividend Policies Matter

Dividend policies are interesting because it affects:

  • Cash returned to shareholders.
  • Cash retained for reinvestment.
  • Signals to the market.
  • Shareholder expectations.

Shareholders may prefer:

  • Stable dividends, or
  • Dividends that reflect actual internal cash needs.

This creates tension between what is financially efficient and what investors expect.

How Dividends Work

UK listed companies typically pay dividends twice per year:

  • Interim dividend mid-year.
  • Final dividend at the year-end.

The board recommends the final dividend, but shareholders vote to approve or reject it at the Annual General Meeting (AGM).

Constraints

  • Dividends may only be paid out of accumulated profits.
  • Restrictions may be imposed by covenants, bank loans, preference share agreements, etc.

Dividend Policy Theories

  • Modigliani & Miller (M&M): Dividend Irrelevance.
  • Dividends as Residual.
  • Clientele Effects.
  • Signalling Theory.
  • Tax Effects.
  • Agency Theory.
  • Share Buybacks & Special Dividends.

These combine to describe why firms choose the dividend patterns they do.

M&M Dividend Irrelevance Hypothesis

M&M argues that dividend policies are irrelevant to shareholder wealth.

Their assumptions:

  • No taxes.
  • No transaction costs.
  • Investors and firms can borrow/lend at the same time.
  • Perfect information.
  • Investors are indifferent between dividends and capital gains.

If true:

  • A shareholder wanting more income can sell shares (homemade dividend).
  • A firm paying dividends can issue new shares to replace cash.

Thus, dividend payout does not affect firm value.

Demonstration:

  • Firm pays all cash flow as dividends.
  • Investment reduces current dividend but creates future cash flows.
  • Whether financed via dividend reduction, or new shares (rights issue).

Shareholder wealth ends up the same.

For example:

An all-equity company which has a policy of paying out all annual net cash flow as dividends. They are expected to generate a net cash flow of £1 million to an infinite horizon. The cost of equity capital is 12%.

PO = 1,000,000 + (1,000,000 / 0.12) = 9,333,000.

A new investment opportunity, which will produce additional cash flows of 180k per year starting in year one. The company will be required to invest £1 million now.

NPV = -1,000,000 + (180,000 / 0.12) = 500,000.

Regardless of whether we sacrifice present dividends, or raise finance through a rights issue, shareholders wealth does in fact not change.

Limitations of M&M

Once we relax the assumptions, dividend policy matters.

Problems with M&M’s world:

  • There are transactions costs.
  • Taxes differ for dividends vs capital gains.
  • Investors cannot access perfect information.
  • Raising external finance is expensive.
  • Managers and investors have conflicting objectives (agency problem).

Therefore, firms face real-world constraints and incentives that make dividend decisions important.

Dividends as a Residual

In a world where external finance is costly, dividends should only be paid after funding all positive NPV projects.

Residual model steps:

  • Identify required investment.
  • Fund all positive NPV projects.
  • Pay out the remainder as dividends.

Implications:

  • Dividends will vary based on investment needs.
  • High-growth firms have low dividends.
  • Mature firms have high dividends.

But shareholders often prefer stable dividends.

Clientele Effects

Different groups of shareholders prefer different dividend patterns:

  • Income-seeking investors prefer high, stable dividends.
  • Growth-seeking investors prefer low dividends and reinvestment.
  • Tax considerations further share preference.

Over time, firms attract a shareholder base (clientele) that prefers their specific dividend pattern. Changing the dividend pattern risks upsetting this clientele.

Taxation Effects

  • Dividends are taxed as income.
  • Capital gains are taxed separately, often at lower rates.

Thus, investors may prefer:

  • Capital gains (lower tax), which is in favour of low dividends.
  • Dividends now – if personal tax circumstances favour income.

This interacts with clientele effects.
