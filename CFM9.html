Managing Foreign Currency Risk

Introduction

Companies often trade internationally, meaning they buy or sell goods/services in currencies different to their home currency. Because exchange rates fluctuate, the value of those transactions (in home currency) becomes uncertain.

Some of the Key Currencies are:

  • UK: Pounds Sterling.
  • Eurozone: Euro.
  • USA: Dollar.
  • Japan: Japanese Yen.

These and other currencies trade on the global FX markets, and their value moves like any other market-based price.

Basic Exchange Rate Arithmetic

Given historical US Dollar vs UK Pound Sterling Rates:

  • Year 1: 2.0774
  • Year 2: 1.3754
  • Year 3: 1.6479
  • Year 4: 1.5497
  • Year 5: 1.5710
  • Year 6: 1.2345

Changes in these values directly affect how many UK pound sterling a company will pay/receive when dealing with US Dollars.

Measuring Currency Performance

To measure the performance of currencies, we use a formula to determine out home currency performance, and foreign currency performance.

Home Currency Performance:

(Closing Rate / Opening Rate) -1

For example: YR1 to YR2:

(1.3754 / 2.0774) -1 = -33.8% (Negative)

I.e., The pound weakened against the dollar by 33.8% in comparison with the year prior.

Foreign Currency Performance:

(Opening Rate / Closing Rate) -1

For example: YR1 to YR2:

(2.0774 / 1.3754) -1 = 51% (Positive)

I.e., The dollar strengthened against the pound by 51% in comparison with the year prior.

Spot Rates: Bid and Offer

A spot rate is the exchange rate for immediate delivery of one currency for another. For example, banks will quote two rates, the bid is the rate that the bank will buy the base currency, and the offer is the rate that the bank sells the base currency. The difference between the banks buy and sell rate is their margin and spread.

The difference - the spead, exists to compensate for the banks market risk, liquidity risk, and admin/profit margins.

For example

US Dollar to UK Pound: Bid of 1.5761, Offer of 1.5765.

Bid = The bank buys currency from you.

Offer = The bank sells the currency to you.

Easy rule is that the customer will always lose the spread, that is always retained by the bank.

Midpoint is: (Bid + Offer) / 2

The Three Types of Foreign Exchange Risk

The three types of risk are:

Transaction Risk:

  • Risk of gain/loss from settling a contract denominated in a foreign currency in the future.
  • Affects operating cash flow.
  • Example: receiving $ later when £ strengthens, means fewer £ received.

Translation Risk:

  • Affects reported values when consolidating accounts of foreign subsidiaries.
  • Does not usually affect cash flow.
  • The impact appears on the Statement of Comprehensive Income.

Economic Risk:

  • Long-term effect on competitiveness caused by exchange rate changes.
  • E.g., if £ strengthens, UK exports become more expensive.

Transaction Risk Example

Exporter Example:

The firm is receiving $2 million in 3 months time. Because the exporter will sell in dollars, we divide using the higher rate (i.e., offer).

Over time:

  • Order Date: £1,271,456
  • Invoice Date: £1,266,624
  • Payment Date: £1,265,022

The £ strengthens and thus we receive less £, i.e., £6,434 lost profit.

Importer Example:

The firm must pay £5 million in 2 months time. Because importer will buy in dollars, we divide using the lower rate (i.e., bid).

Over time:

  • Order Date: £3,182,686
  • Invoice Date: £3,172,588
  • Payment Date: £3,168,567

The £ strengthened and thus we required less £. I.e., £14,119 cheaper.

Managing Exchange Risk (Treasury Function)

A good treasury system requires:

  • Real-Time currency information.
  • Net currency position analysis.
  • Hedging Policy.
  • Speculation Policy (whether the firm should speculate on FX movements).

Hedging Tools (External Markets)

The three major external hedges are:

Forward Contracts

  • Lock in a rate today for a transaction in the future.
  • Tailored contracts.
  • No cash exchanged until settlement date.

Money Market Hedge

  • Uses borrowing/lending in money markets to create a natural hedge.

Derivatives

  • Futures.
  • Options.
  • Swaps.

Hedging - Exporter Scenario

Information:

  • The importer must pay 10 million Hong Kong currency in 3-months time.
  • Spot is: (Bid) 12.9530 and (Offer) 12.9988
  • Forward 3-months: (Bid) 12.7884 and (Offer) 12.8484
  • UK Interest Borrow at 7% with a 5% deposit.
  • KH Interest Borrow at 6% with a 4% deposit.

Option 1 - Forward Contract

Becuase the exporter is selling foreign currnecy, use offer rate of 12.8484, therefore, (10,000,000 / 12.8484) = 778,307.

Option 2 - Money Market Hedge

Step 1: borrow foreign currency: 6% / 4 = 1.5%, therefore, (10,000,000 x 1.015) = 9,852,217.

Step 2: convert to £ (the offer): (9,852,217 / 12.9988) = 757,933.

Step 3: invest in UK: 7% / 3 = 1.25%, therefore, (757,933 x 1.0125) = 767,407.

If it's used to reduce UK borrowng: 7% / 4 = 1.75%, therefore, (757,933 x 1.0175) = 771,196.

Option 3 - Lead Payment (encourage early payment)

Step 1: Give 2% discount: receive (10,000,000 x 98%) = 9,800,000.

Step 2: Convert to spot: (9,800,000 / 12.9988) = 753,915.

Step 3: Invest in UK: 5% / 4 = 1.25, therefore, (753,915 x 1.0125) = 763,339.

If it's used to reduce UK borrowing: 7% / 4 = 1.75, therefore, (753,915 x 1.0175) = 767,108.

In conclusion

  • Option 1: Forward Contract: 778,307.
  • Option 2: Money Market Hedge: 767,407.
  • Option 3: Lead Payment: 763,339.

Option 1 offers the highest return; thus, we with this that option in this scenario.

More Information on options 2 and 3

Information

The UK exporter will receive 10 million Hong Kong currency in 3 months time, but that currency may weaken between now and them, therefore, a money market hedge is where we 'lock in' the today home currency value of Hong Kong value (referred to as 'Receipt') using: borrowing, conversion, and investment. This removes the exchange rate uncertainty completely.

Option 2: Money Market Hedge

In this option, there are three steps, with the last being based on how we'd like to use that money.

Step 1 is to borrow the forign currency, Step 2 is to convert the foreign currency to home currency, and Step 3 is how we're going to use it.

Step 1: the borrow.

The idea here is to borrow, but we know that we'll be incurring interest, therefore, if we borrow the present value today, in three months time, the borrowed amount (principal) and the added interest, would make up the 10 million we're set to receive. This means that in three months when we do receive the 10 million, it's automatically paid off as the interest is embedded in the final payment amount (the receipt).

Step 2: the conversion.

The purpose of converting the foreign currency immediately is to lock in its home currency value. If conversion were delayed, movements in the exchange rate could result in fewer home currency being received, whereas early conversion fixes the amount with certainty.

Step 3: the spending.

The converted funds may be used either to reduce existing domestic borrowing, generating a return equal to the interest saved, or placed on short-term deposit to earn interest during the hedging period. I.e., aim is to use it to either reduce interest costs or increase interest income.

Option 3: Lead Payment

In this option, there are three steps too, with the last being based on how we'd like to use that money.

Step 1 is to offer a discount, Step 2 is to convert the foreign currency to home currency, and Step 3 is how we're going to use it.

Step 1: the discount.

The aim is to encourage early payment by the customer by offering a discount (reducing sales revenue). This is a way to accelerate the cash inflow, but it shortens period of exposure to FX risk, but only if the exchange rate does not change negatively between when agreement it made, and payment date.

Step 2: the conversion.

The purpose of converting the foreign currency immediately is to lock in its home currency value. If conversion were delayed, movements in the exchange rate could result in fewer home currency being received, whereas early conversion fixes the amount with certainty.

Step 3: the spending.

The converted funds may be used either to reduce existing domestic borrowing, generating a return equal to the interest saved, or placed on short-term deposit to earn interest during the hedging period. I.e., aim is to use it to either reduce interest costs or increase interest income.

Internal Hedging Methods (No External Markets)

Netting & Matching:

Used to offset receivables and payables within a group so only the net amount is exchanged. A company has a subsidiary in each Hong Kong, Germany, and UK – net off using a single currency.

For example: we’ll use UK sterling with a UK company being the parent, and the others being subsidiaries.

  • HK owes UK 500k
  • Germany owes HK 1,200k
  • UK owes Germany 600k

Exchange rate is: £1 = 12 HKD and 1.20 Euro.

We need to convert these to UK Stering as UK company is the parent:

  • Becuase HK owes UK 500k, that's already in UK sterling, so we leave that as is.
  • Germany owes (in HKD) HK 1.2 million, so we convert HKD to UK Sterling: (1.2m / 12) = 100k
  • UK owes (in Euro) Germany 600k, so we convert Euro to UK Sterling: (600k / 1.20) = 500k.

We then need to move the net amounts in a single transaction to prevent making multiple transactions. Therefore:

  • HK owes UK 500k and is due 100k from Germany, makes net -400k (HK).
  • UK owes HK 500k and is due 500k from Germany, makes net 0.
  • Germany owes HK 100k and is due 500k from UK, makes net +400k (Germany).

The net position is settled by HK paying Germany 400k. i.e, net is 0 across the board.

On that note, remember: negative net = payer and positive net = receiver.

Therefore, we've reduced 3 transactions to 1 transaction by exchanging the net amounts.

Lead/Lag Payments

  • Lead: pay early if expecting exchange rate to worsen.
  • Lag: delay payment if expecting rate to improve.

Theories of Exchange Rate Movements

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

Currencies adjust to keep prices equal between countries. For example:

  • UK Price = £1,000
  • KH Price = 12,000
  • Inflation = UK 2%, HK 6%
  • Future UK Price: 1,000 x 1.02 = 1,020.
  • Future HK Price: 12,000 x 1.06 = 12,720.
  • Future Exchange Rate: 12,720 / 1,020 = 12.47.

Interest Rate Parity (IRP)

Interest rates differ because of expected inflation, so currencies with higher interest rates tend to weaken.

Formula: Future Rate = Spot x (1 + foreign interest rate) / (1 + home interest rate).

For example:

  • Spot: 1.6500.
  • Australia Interest: 1%.
  • UK Interest: 0.125%
  • Forward = 1.6500 x (1.01 / 1.00125) = 1.6644

Converting back after 3 months gives essentially the same £ as investing in the UK > not arbitrage.
